4.32 Asset Lineage Policy

4.32.1 Purpose

The Asset Lineage Policy defines how relationships between assets are created, preserved, and verified across worlds, versions, timelines, and multiverse contexts.

If provenance is the story of an asset, lineage is the family tree — the constitutional record of how assets evolve, fork, merge, descend, and relate to one another.

Lineage ensures that:

versions remain connected

forks remain traceable

merges remain accountable

descendants remain anchored

worlds cannot rewrite ancestry

canonical truth survives across timelines

Without lineage, digital reality becomes untraceable.

4.32.2 Constitutional Requirements

1. Canonical Lineage Graph

Every asset must maintain a canonical lineage graph that records:

parent assets

child assets

sibling relationships

forks

merges

world migrations

resurrection ancestry

deprecation ancestry

This graph must be:

immutable

cryptographically anchored

reconstructable

world‑agnostic

Lineage is not a suggestion — it is constitutional truth.

2. Lineage Must Be Deterministic

Given the same ancestry, the lineage graph must always reconstruct identically.

No world may introduce nondeterministic lineage behavior.

3. Lineage Cannot Be Rewritten

No actor — world, creator, or system — may:

remove ancestors

alter parentage

forge descendants

hide forks

collapse branches

merge without constitutional rules

Lineage is permanent.

4. Lineage Must Survive All Transitions

Lineage must remain intact across:

world migrations

cross‑world interactions

multiverse transitions

resurrection events

compliance transformations

disaster recovery

export/import operations

Lineage is the genetic memory of the asset.

4.32.3 Lineage Event Types

Lineage must record:

Genesis — the asset’s origin

Versioning — new versions, branches, or revisions

Forks — divergent evolution paths

Merges — constitutional merging of branches

Descendants — assets derived from others

World migrations — lineage continuity across worlds

Resurrection ancestry — canonical restoration after loss

Deprecation ancestry — end‑of‑life lineage

Each event must be:

hashed

signed

linked to provenance

linked to integrity updates

Lineage and provenance are inseparable.

4.32.4 Forking Rules

A fork occurs when:

an asset evolves into multiple independent branches

a world applies divergent rules

a creator intentionally splits evolution paths

Forks must:

be explicitly logged

maintain shared ancestry

never overwrite each other

remain independently verifiable

Forks are constitutional, not accidental.

4.32.5 Merging Rules

A merge occurs when:

two or more branches converge

a world reconciles divergent versions

a creator unifies evolution paths

Merges must:

be explicitly logged

preserve all ancestry

never collapse history

produce a new canonical integrityHash

be verifiable by recomputing all parent branches

Merges cannot erase the past.

4.32.6 Resurrection Lineage

When an asset is resurrected:

its lineage must reference the last known canonical ancestor

resurrection must not create a new identity

resurrection must not break ancestry

resurrection must be logged as a lineage event

Resurrection is continuity, not replacement.

4.32.7 Lineage Violations

Any attempt to:

rewrite ancestry

forge parentage

hide forks

collapse branches

merge without constitutional rules

break lineage continuity

…constitutes a lineage violation.

Violations must:

be logged

trigger zero‑trust enforcement

invalidate dependent provenance claims

escalate to Platform Governance

Lineage violations are treated as attempts to falsify history.

4.32.8 Constitutional Principle

Lineage is the ancestry of truth.If lineage breaks, reality fractures.
