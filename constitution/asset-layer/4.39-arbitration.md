4.39 Asset Multiverse Conflict Resolution & Canonical Arbitration Policy

4.39.1 Purpose

The Asset Multiverse Conflict Resolution & Canonical Arbitration Policy defines how the Berry Platform™ resolves conflicts involving asset identity, provenance, lineage, integrity, custody, or state across multiple worlds, timelines, or federated systems.

This policy ensures that no world, system, or actor can unilaterally rewrite truth.

Arbitration is the constitutional mechanism that maintains canonical coherence across the multiverse.

4.39.2 Why Arbitration Exists

Conflicts arise when:

worlds apply divergent rules

versions evolve independently

forks collide

merges disagree

custody claims conflict

provenance diverges

lineage branches contradict

resurrection events produce ambiguity

compliance transformations differ across jurisdictions

external systems introduce incompatible states

Without arbitration, truth fractures.

4.39.3 Constitutional Requirements

1. Canonical Truth Cannot Be World‑Specific

No world may define canonical truth.Worlds may interpret truth, but they cannot:

override identity

rewrite provenance

collapse lineage

forge custody

alter integrity

suppress history

Canonical truth is defined at the platform level, not the world level.

2. Arbitration Must Be Deterministic

Given the same evidence, arbitration must always produce the same canonical result.

No nondeterministic behavior.No world‑specific outcomes.No subjective interpretation.

3. Arbitration Must Be Evidence‑Based

Arbitration decisions must rely on:

identityHash

provenance chain

lineage graph

integrityHash

custody chain

cryptographic signatures

world‑level logs

multiverse context

Arbitration is forensic, not political.

4.39.4 Types of Conflicts

Arbitration must resolve:

1. Identity Conflicts

Two worlds claim different identities for the same asset.

2. Provenance Conflicts

Provenance chains diverge or contradict.

3. Lineage Conflicts

Forks, merges, or ancestry differ across worlds.

4. Integrity Conflicts

IntegrityHash mismatches or tampering is detected.

5. Custody Conflicts

Multiple actors claim control.

6. Version Conflicts

Divergent versions cannot be reconciled locally.

7. Resurrection Conflicts

Multiple resurrection attempts produce inconsistent states.

8. Compliance Conflicts

Jurisdictions require different representational transformations.

9. Multiverse Conflicts

Cross‑world interactions produce incompatible truths.

Arbitration is the constitutional referee.

4.39.5 Arbitration Process

A valid arbitration must:

Collect all evidence

Verify identity

Recompute provenance

Recompute lineage

Recompute integrity

Validate signatures

Detect tampering or omissions

Determine canonical truth

Log the arbitration decision

Update all dependent systems

Arbitration is a forensic reconstruction of truth.

4.39.6 Arbitration Outcomes

Arbitration may result in:

1. Canonical Acceptance

One version is declared canonical.

2. Canonical Merge

Multiple branches are merged into a single canonical state.

3. Canonical Fork

Branches remain separate but are formally recognized.

4. Canonical Override

Tampered or invalid branches are rejected.

5. Canonical Resurrection

A lost or corrupted asset is restored.

6. Canonical Redaction

Compliance requires representational transformation.

7. Canonical Nullification

A fraudulent or invalid asset is constitutionally voided.

Arbitration does not guess — it determines.

4.39.7 Arbitration Violations

Any attempt to:

bypass arbitration

suppress evidence

forge signatures

alter identity

rewrite provenance

collapse lineage

fabricate custody

override canonical truth at the world level

…constitutes an arbitration violation.

Violations must:

be logged

trigger zero‑trust enforcement

escalate to Platform Governance

invalidate dependent claims

Arbitration violations are treated as attempts to falsify reality.

4.39.8 Constitutional Principle

Arbitration protects truth from divergence.If arbitration fails, the multiverse collapses into contradiction.
