4.44 Asset Deletion, Redaction & Right‑to‑Be‑Forgotten Policy

4.44.1 Purpose

The Asset Deletion, Redaction & Right‑to‑Be‑Forgotten Policy defines how the Berry Platform™ handles requests for deletion, privacy, redaction, and legal erasure without compromising identity, provenance, lineage, integrity, or constitutional truth.

Berry does not delete truth.Berry does not rewrite history.Berry does not collapse lineage.

Instead, Berry introduces a constitutional model:

Deletion is representational, not destructive.

4.44.2 Why Deletion Must Be Constitutional

Traditional systems satisfy deletion requests by:

erasing data

overwriting history

collapsing lineage

destroying evidence

mutating identity

breaking continuity

These actions violate digital truth.

Berry solves this by separating:

canonical truth (immutable)

representational visibility (mutable)

This allows Berry to honor privacy and legal requirements without falsifying reality.

4.44.3 Constitutional Requirements

1. Deletion Must Not Destroy Truth

Deletion may:

hide data

restrict access

remove visibility

replace content with placeholders

obfuscate sensitive fields

…but deletion may not:

erase canonical history

alter identity

rewrite provenance

collapse lineage

break integrity

fabricate replacement data

Truth is immutable even when visibility changes.

2. Deletion Must Be Logged

Every deletion or redaction event must:

be logged in provenance

update integrityHash

include actor identity

include legal basis

include jurisdiction

be cryptographically signed

Deletion is never silent.

3. Deletion Must Be Reversible

Reversibility means:

auditors can verify canonical truth

the system can reconstruct the pre‑deletion state

lineage remains intact

integrity remains verifiable

Reversibility does not mean restoring visibility — it means preserving truth.

4. Deletion Must Be Representational

Deletion transforms what is shown, not what is true.

Representational deletion may include:

nullification

masking

obfuscation

placeholder substitution

access restriction

jurisdiction‑specific views

Canonical truth remains intact beneath the representation.

4.44.4 Types of Deletion & Redaction

1. Soft Deletion

The asset becomes invisible but remains canonical.

2. Hard Redaction

Specific fields are replaced with constitutional placeholders.

3. Jurisdictional Redaction

Different regions see different representations.

4. Representational Deletion

The asset appears deleted, but canonical truth is preserved.

5. Constitutional Nullification

The asset is declared void but its history remains intact.

Berry never destroys truth — it contextualizes it.

4.44.5 Right‑to‑Be‑Forgotten Implementation

When a user invokes the right‑to‑be‑forgotten:

personal data is redacted

visibility is removed

access is restricted

representational deletion is applied

canonical truth is preserved

provenance logs the event

integrityHash updates

lineage remains intact

Berry honors human rights without falsifying history.

4.44.6 Deletion Triggers

Deletion or redaction may be triggered by:

user privacy requests

GDPR or CCPA requirements

legal orders

compliance mandates

world‑level policy

creator intent

institutional governance

All triggers must be logged and justified.

4.44.7 Deletion Violations

Any attempt to:

delete canonical history

rewrite identity

collapse lineage

alter provenance

bypass logging

fabricate replacement data

perform silent deletion

destroy evidence

…constitutes a deletion violation.

Violations must:

be logged

trigger zero‑trust enforcement

escalate to Platform Governance

invalidate dependent claims

Deletion violations are treated as attempts to falsify reality.

4.44.8 Constitutional Principle

Deletion changes visibility, not truth.Redaction protects privacy, not fiction.Truth must survive even when memory must fade.
