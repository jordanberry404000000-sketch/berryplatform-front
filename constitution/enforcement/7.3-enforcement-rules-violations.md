7.3 Enforcement Rules & Violations

7.3.1 Purpose

This chapter defines the rules Berry Platform™ enforces and the violations that trigger enforcement actions.Rules are the backbone of institutional order.Violations are the signals that order has been challenged.

Enforcement is not arbitrary — it is rule‑bound, constitutional, and verifiable.

7.3.2 Rule Structure

Every enforcement rule must include:

Rule Identifier — unique, immutable, canonical

Scope — actor, world, asset, system, governance

Authority Requirements — who may enforce it

Violation Conditions — what triggers enforcement

Enforcement Actions — what happens when violated

Evidence Requirements — what must be logged

Escalation Path — where the violation goes next

Rules must be deterministic and reproducible.

7.3.3 Rule Categories

Berry recognizes five categories of enforcement rules:

1. Integrity Rules

Rules that protect canonical truth.

Violations include:

tampering with chains

modifying canonical data

forging signatures

bypassing verification logic

Integrity violations are treated as constitutional threats.

2. Authority Rules

Rules that govern who may do what.

Violations include:

enforcing without authority

exceeding authority scope

impersonating authority

forging authority credentials

Authority violations undermine institutional legitimacy.

3. Custody & Lineage Rules

Rules that govern asset movement and provenance.

Violations include:

unauthorized custody changes

broken lineage transitions

invalid asset mutations

custody without proof

Custody violations threaten asset truth.

4. Governance Rules

Rules that govern governance actions and rulings.

Violations include:

ignoring governance rulings

fabricating governance actions

suppressing governance logs

unauthorized role changes

Governance violations are treated as high‑severity breaches.

5. System Rules

Rules that govern runtime, environment, and system behavior.

Violations include:

sandbox escape attempts

unauthorized process injection

tampering with verification logic

manipulating system state

System violations indicate adversarial behavior.

7.3.4 Violation Classification

Violations are classified into three levels:

Level 1 — Local Violations

Impact limited to a single actor, world, or asset.Handled by local enforcement.

Examples:

invalid signatures

unauthorized local actions

minor custody errors

Level 2 — Structural Violations

Impact affects system boundaries or cross‑world behavior.Requires system enforcement.

Examples:

cross‑world authority misuse

chain inconsistencies

unauthorized system‑level actions

Level 3 — Constitutional Violations

Impact threatens canonical truth, governance integrity, or institutional stability.Requires governance enforcement.

Examples:

forging governance actions

tampering with canonical truth

compromising verification logic

authority impersonation

Constitutional violations are existential threats.

7.3.5 Violation Requirements

A violation must include:

actor identity

action payload

violation type

rule identifier

evidence snapshot

timestamps

world‑context

chain state

Violations must be non‑repudiable.

7.3.6 Violation Outcomes

Every violation must result in one of:

rejection

quarantine

suspension

revocation

sanction

escalation

No violation may be ignored or suppressed.

7.3.7 Violation Suppression

Any attempt to:

hide violations

delete violation logs

prevent enforcement

alter evidence

suppress escalation

…is itself a constitutional violation.

Violation suppression is treated as hostile behavior.

7.3.8 Constitutional Principle

Rules define order.Violations define threat.Enforcement defines survival.
