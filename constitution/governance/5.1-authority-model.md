5.1 Authority Model

5.1.1 Purpose

The Authority Model defines the constitutional framework governing the delegation, limitation, and verification of authority within the Berry Platform™.It establishes who may perform actions, under what conditions, and subject to which constraints, ensuring that no actor—human, system, or world—may exercise power beyond what the Constitution explicitly permits.

Authority is not assumed.Authority is not inherited.Authority is not implicit.Authority must be granted, verified, and enforced.

5.1.2 Authority Categories

Berry recognizes four constitutionally distinct categories of authority:

1. Creator Authority

Authority granted to the original creator(s) of an asset or system component.Creators may initiate, define, and extend canonical truth, subject to constitutional constraints.

2. Custodial Authority

Authority granted to entities responsible for holding, managing, or transferring assets.Custodians may not alter identity, provenance, lineage, or integrity.

3. Governance Authority

Authority granted to governance bodies responsible for oversight, enforcement, and adjudication.Governance authority is supervisory, not creative.

4. System Authority

Authority exercised by automated or autonomous processes operating under constitutional rules.System authority must be deterministic, verifiable, and non‑discretionary.

No actor may hold more than one category of authority unless explicitly granted by governance statute.

5.1.3 Authority Requirements

All authority within Berry must satisfy the following constitutional requirements:

1. Explicit Grant

Authority must be explicitly defined and granted.No actor may claim authority by implication, assumption, or precedent.

2. Cryptographic Verification

Authority must be verifiable through:

signatures

identityHash

custody chain

governance records

role assignments

If authority cannot be verified, it does not exist.

3. Least‑Privilege Enforcement

Actors may only exercise the minimum authority required for their role.Excess authority is unconstitutional.

4. Non‑Destructive Constraint

Authority may not be used to:

alter identity

rewrite provenance

collapse lineage

bypass integrity

fabricate history

Authority governs actions, not truth.

5.1.4 Authority Boundaries

Authority is limited by:

constitutional rules

role definitions

provenance constraints

integrity enforcement

governance oversight

zero‑trust assumptions

No actor may exceed constitutional boundaries, regardless of role or intent.

5.1.5 Authority Escalation

When an action exceeds an actor’s authority:

The action must be rejected.

The attempt must be logged.

The event must be escalated to Governance.

Zero‑trust enforcement must be applied.

Dependent actions must be invalidated.

Escalation is mandatory, not discretionary.

5.1.6 Authority Revocation

Authority may be revoked when:

an actor violates constitutional rules

governance determines misuse

custody changes

a role is terminated

a system becomes compromised

Revocation must be:

logged

signed

verifiable

irreversible without governance approval

Authority is conditional, not permanent.

5.1.7 Authority Violations

Any attempt to:

exercise unauthorized authority

fabricate authority

exceed granted permissions

bypass verification

suppress authority logs

impersonate an authorized actor

override constitutional constraints

…constitutes an authority violation.

Violations must trigger:

logging

enforcement

escalation

adjudication

Authority violations are treated as attempts to seize unconstitutional power.

5.1.8 Constitutional Principle

Authority must be granted, not assumed.Authority must be verified, not trusted.Authority must be constrained, not absolute.
